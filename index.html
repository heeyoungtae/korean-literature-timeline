1905
Eulsa Treaty (을사조약) →
The Eulsa Treaty (also known as the Japan-Korea Protectorate Treaty) was signed on November 17, 1905, effectively stripping Korea of its diplomatic sovereignty. The treaty was signed under duress by five Korean ministers (the "Five Eulsa Traitors") without Emperor Gojong's approval. This marked a critical step toward full Japanese annexation in 1910. The treaty led to the establishment of the Japanese Residency-General, with Itō Hirobumi as the first Resident-General.
Primary sources: Treaty text Imperial documents
Secondary sources: Duus, "The Abacus and the Sword" Schmid, "Korea Between Empires" Robinson, "Korea's Twentieth-Century Odyssey"
1906
Yi In-jik, Tears of Blood (혈의 누) →
Korea's first modern novel, serialized in the newspaper Mansebo. Written by Yi In-jik, it tells the story of a Korean woman separated from her family during the turmoil of foreign invasions. The novel introduced Western narrative techniques and the concept of the "new novel" (신소설, shinsoseol) to Korean literature, marking a departure from classical Korean fiction. It reflected the influence of Japanese and Western literature while addressing contemporary political concerns about Korea's sovereignty.
Translations: English translation (Columbia UP)
Critical studies: Lee, "The Birth of Modern Korean Literature" Kim, "New Novel and Modernity"
Context: Shinsoseol movement
1907
Hague Secret Emissary Affair →
Emperor Gojong secretly dispatched three envoys (Yi Sang-seol, Yi Jun, and Yi Wi-jong) to The Hague Peace Conference to protest the illegitimacy of the 1905 Eulsa Treaty and appeal for international support. The mission failed as the envoys were denied entry to the conference. Japan used this as a pretext to force Gojong's abdication in favor of his son, who became Emperor Sunjong. This event led to the dissolution of the Korean army and increased Japanese control.
Primary sources: Emissary letters Conference records
Secondary sources: Larsen, "Korean Independence Movements" Dudden, "Japan's Colonization of Korea"
1908
Ch'oe Nam-sŏn, "From the Sea to Children" (해에게서 소년에게) →
Published in the inaugural issue of the youth magazine Sonyŏn (少年), this is recognized as the first modern Korean poem written in vernacular Korean rather than classical Chinese. Ch'oe Nam-sŏn's work broke from traditional poetic forms and used free verse to express ideas about youth, vitality, and enlightenment. The poem's bold imagery and nationalist undertones inspired a generation of Korean writers during the colonial period.
Text: Korean text with annotations English translation
Criticism: Kim Yoon-shik, "Modern Korean Poetry" McCann, "Early Korean Literature"

1910
Japan-Korea Annexation Treaty →
On August 22, 1910, the Japan-Korea Annexation Treaty was signed, formally ending the Korean Empire and beginning 35 years of Japanese colonial rule. The treaty was signed by Prime Minister Yi Wan-yong and Japanese Resident-General Terauchi Masatake. Korea became a colony of Japan, known as Chōsen (朝鮮), administered by the Governor-General of Korea. This period saw systematic suppression of Korean culture, language, and identity, along with economic exploitation and land dispossession.
Documents: Annexation treaty text Emperor Sunjong's final edict
Historiography: Eckert, "Korea Old and New" Schmid, "Korea Between Empires" Robinson, "Cultural Nationalism in Colonial Korea"
Debates: Treaty legitimacy controversy

1917
Yi Kwang-su, The Heartless (무정) →
Landmark modern novel serialized in the Maeil Sinbo newspaper. Explores themes of enlightenment, nationalism, and individual freedom during the colonial period. Yi Kwang-su's narrative technique and psychological realism set new standards for Korean fiction and influenced generations of writers.
Translations: English translation (University of Hawaiʻi Press)
Critical studies: Kim Chul, "Yi Kwang-su and Modern Korean Literature" Lee, "The Colonial Context"

1919
March 1st Movement (3·1 운동) →
Nationwide independence demonstrations against Japanese colonial rule, sparked by the reading of the Korean Declaration of Independence on March 1, 1919. Over two million Koreans participated in more than 1,500 demonstrations. The Japanese response was brutal, with thousands killed and tens of thousands arrested. The movement led to the establishment of the Korean Provisional Government in Shanghai and influenced colonial policy.
Primary sources: Declaration of Independence Japanese colonial records
Scholarship: Lee, "The March First Movement" Park, "National Identity and Korean Nationalism"
Creation (창조) magazine founded →
First Korean literary journal dedicated to promoting modern literature. Founded by Kim Dong-in, Ju Yo-han, and others. The journal introduced Western literary trends and modernist aesthetics to Korean readers and writers.
Sources: Creation magazine archive
Context: Kim, "Literary Magazines in Colonial Korea"
